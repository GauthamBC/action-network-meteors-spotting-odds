More Americans are chasing unforgettable night-sky experiences, but every night spent waiting for a meteor is still a gamble. You might catch a fireball blazing across the sky, or spend an hour craning your neck in a dark parking lot only to drive home empty-handed.

And this summer’s slate is stacked. The Delta Aquariids and Alpha Capricornids are already streaking through July skies, and the headliner Perseids peak under a moonless August sky. So Action Network did what it does best: turned that uncertainty into odds.

Our odds experts calculated each state’s implied probability and moneyline using six key factors: dark-sky zones, clear days, recorded fireball activity, observatories, DarkSky-certified places and elevation. Think of it as an odds board for the night sky. Is your state a strong pick for a spectacular sighting, or are you taking your chances every time you look up?

Top 10 States: The Overall Index

Ranked by implied spotting probability, created from the six core factors and their weightings.

The top of the index is a Western story. Nine of the ten leaders sit west of the Mississippi, where dry air and dark public land stack the deck. California leads at a 7.34% implied spotting probability, four and a half times the national median, pairing the country’s most fireball reports with 39 zones rated Bortle Class 1 or 2, the darkest classes on the scale, while Arizona delivers 197 clear days a year and Utah has certified more dark-sky places than any state. Michigan is the East’s lone gate-crasher at No. 9, lifted by Upper Peninsula darkness and more than 1,000 fireball reports.

The middle of the pack is where one elite metric collides with one stubborn gap. Florida files the third-most fireball reports in the country from barely above sea level, Pennsylvania and New York pair famous dark-sky escapes and deep observatory benches with heavy light pollution, and Alaska owns more Bortle Class 1 skies than any state yet manages only about 60 clear nights a year. Each has a case for the top 10; each has the one number that keeps it out.

At the bottom, the Northeast’s clouds and city glow do most of the damage. Vermont closes the table at a 0.61% implied spotting probability with the fewest clear nights anywhere, 58 a year, and Delaware has no listed zone darker than Bortle Class 4. The consolation for the long shots is that the sky does not read the rankings: North Dakota files the fewest fireball reports in the country and still owns the 90-kilogram Richardton fall of 1918.

“For Americans planning to watch the Perseids, the key takeaway is that your state sets the opening line, but it does not decide the outcome. California leads with a 7.34% implied spotting probability because it combines more of the conditions meteor watchers need, but cloud cover, wildfire smoke and local visibility can quickly change the outlook on any given night. The rankings show where viewers have the strongest starting position, not where a sighting is guaranteed.”

Methodology & Sources

Action Network’s odds experts created each state’s implied spotting probability and American moneyline odds from the six core factors below and their weightings. Each factor was rescaled using min–max normalization, a standard method that puts every metric on the same 0–1 scale, and the probability determines the final ranking.

Bortle Class 1–3 dark-sky zones30%
Annual clear days25%
Fireball events20%
Observatory count10%
DarkSky-certified places10%
Average elevation5%

Sources & notes: Dark-sky zones and Bortle classes: Go-Astronomy site lists (Bortle class reflects location potential; actual conditions vary with weather, smoke, and humidity). Clear days: NOAA NCEI comparative climatic data, daylight-hour cloud cover, and state means across listed stations. Fireball events: American Meteor Society unique state-event pairs, 2021–2025. Observatories: Go-Astronomy state pages. Certified places: DarkSky U.S. places list. Elevation: validated state mean elevations. Meteorite history: NASA meteorite landings archive, witnessed falls.
